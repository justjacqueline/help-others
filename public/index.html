How can you help others?

There are many ways to do good. Some programs have especially strong evidence behind them and can have a very high impact per dollar — meaning each dollar goes further in saving lives or improving well-being. This tool explores four charities recommended by GiveWell, showing how the same donation can achieve very different outcomes depending on where and how it's used (and these are already well-studied global health programs). Click any cell to explore what a $1M donation achieves.

Data from GiveWell's cost-effectiveness models

To compare and rank them, we need some way to put those outcomes on a common scale. That is very difficult, because it requires judging how much different outcomes matter relative to one another. These relative judgments are called moral weights.

GiveWell has put a lot of thought into how it defines and regularly updates its moral weights. This page lets you explore how different moral weights can change the ranking of options.

(This idea can also be useful when comparing across causes, such as when considering how much value to assign to helping people, animals, or the environment.)

Explore different moral weights

Imagine someone living in extreme poverty on about $1,000 a year. You could double their everyday needs to $2,000 — for one person, for one year. How many people would need that improvement for it to feel as valuable as saving one life?

If you enter 100 for "Under 5," saving one child under 5 feels as valuable as giving 100 people that one-year improvement.
